📋 Credible Source Checklist

Is This Source Legit?

Use this checklist every time you research online. Hover over any item to reveal what to do if the answer is NO.

🔗
Section 1: URL & Domain
Ask yourself: What does the web address tell me?
👀 Look at: your browser's address bar
✓
I checked the domain extension (.gov, .edu, .org, .com)
💡 .gov and .edu are restricted — usually more trustworthy than .com or .org
❌ If NO —
Look up who owns the site at whois.domaintools.com before trusting it
✓
The URL looks clean — no random numbers, weird symbols, or misspelled brand names
💡 Sketchy URLs are a red flag — trust your gut!
❌ If NO —
Stop here. Fake sites copy real URLs with small typos (e.g. "amaz0n.com"). Find a different source
✓
I didn't just trust the domain — I also checked the actual content
💡 A .org site can still be misleading (e.g. passportUSA.org)
❌ If NO —
Scroll through the whole page before deciding if you can trust it
🧑‍💼
Section 2: Author & Credibility
Ask yourself: Who wrote this — and should I trust them?
👀 Look at: "About Us," author byline, footer
✓
I can find the name of the author or organization behind this site
💡 Check the "About Us" page or footer — anonymous sources are suspicious
❌ If NO —
No author = major red flag. Anonymous sources are not trustworthy. Find a different source
✓
The author's background or experience actually matches the topic
💡 A doctor writing about medicine = trustworthy. A random blogger = be cautious
❌ If NO —
Look for a qualified expert on your topic instead — credentials should match the subject
✓
I Googled the author to confirm they are who they say they are
💡 2 minutes of Googling can save you from using a fake expert!
❌ If NO results —
Real experts have an online presence. Zero results = suspicious. Keep digging
🔍
Section 3: Accuracy & Quality
Ask yourself: Can I actually trust what this site is saying?
👀 Look at: article body, links, footnotes
✓
There are no major spelling, grammar, or punctuation errors
💡 Lots of errors = low effort = the information probably wasn't carefully checked either
❌ If NO —
Don't use it. Errors suggest the content wasn't reviewed for accuracy either. Move on
✓
The site links to other reputable sources to support its claims
💡 Credible sites show their evidence — like footnotes in a textbook
❌ If NO —
No evidence = unproven claims. Look for sources that back up what they say with real facts
✓
I found the same information on at least one other trustworthy source
💡 If only one site says it, be very skeptical!
❌ If NO —
Keep searching. If only ONE site says it, the information may be false or made up
⚖️
Section 4: Bias & Purpose
Ask yourself: Is this site trying to influence me?
👀 Look at: headlines, ads, tone of writing
✓
The site presents balanced information — not just one strong opinion
💡 One-sided articles and opinionated blogs are signs of bias
❌ If NO —
Note the bias in your notes box and find a more balanced source to compare it with
✓
The headline doesn't use clickbait words like "SHOCKING," "You won't believe..." or "ALARMING"
💡 Clickbait is designed to get clicks — not to inform you
❌ If NO —
Skip it. Sensational headlines usually mean the content prioritizes drama over facts
✓
Any ads on the page don't push a specific viewpoint or try to sell me something related to the content
💡 Ads that match the article's opinion = possible paid bias
❌ If NO —
Ask yourself: is this site trying to change my mind or make money? That affects its reliability
📅
Section 5: Currency & Relevance
Ask yourself: Is this information up to date and useful for my topic?
👀 Look at: page footer, article top, copyright date
✓
I can find a publication or "last updated" date on the page
💡 No date = no way to know if this is outdated information
❌ If NO —
Try to find a dated version of the same information on a different site
✓
The information is recent enough for my topic (especially science, health, or current events)
💡 A 2009 article about COVID-19 is useless — always check the date!
❌ If NO —
Old information can be dangerously wrong on fast-changing topics. Search for a more recent article
✓
The content directly answers or relates to my research question
💡 A great source on the wrong topic still doesn't help your research!
❌ If NO —
Refine your search terms and try again — relevance is just as important as credibility
